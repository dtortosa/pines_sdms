Below is a short summary of the most important points that popped up in various parts of the manuscript as comments: 

                1) We once discussed a similar idea of using the reconstructed ancestral niche for "correcting future projections" with Nicolas Salamin (Univ. Lausanne), with whom we have often collaborated regarding evolutionary analyses. He believes it is ok to assume that the difference between the MRCA and the current niche has information for the adaptation potential, but he strongly believes that it is not ok to assume that converting the niche back towards that of the MRCA is more likely (easier) than towards the opposite of this direction. You can either correct for this, or - if not - you should discuss that this is a very strong assumption (for which there is likely no good proof). You will also see that we find the way you calculated the correction is not clear and understandable. 

                2) You integrate the many model/data projections (36 for current, 1008 for future conditions per spp) into a compound index, which you describe as "suitability". We find this an inappropriate description of what it expresses. It basically expresses the degree of uncertainty that the models predict a pixel as "fully suitable". So you mix probability of occurrence with uncertainty of occurrence in your description, and this confusion has a strong impact on the presentation of results and the followup discussion. We believe it is important that you first decide, what uncertainty is acceptable (e.g. 75% agreement of presence required) and then treat this as "simulated presence"). You do no longer analyse "suitability" that comes from probability. Instead, you have already classified each single model, and then averaged these predictions. This is uncertainty. And - as I mention in the introduction - you do not correct for this uncertainty. You visualize it. 

                3) Linked to point 2) you have a very specific (uncommon) way of calculating the effect of climate change on "suitabilities" and "ranges" of pines. This makes it even more complicated to structure the discussion (we felt). Traditionally, one calculates two measures under climate change: "range loss" and "range change". Range loss is the degree to which the current range becomes unsuitable (which proportion of the current range does not remain suitable but actually loses suitability. A range loss of 50% means that 50% of the current range become unsuitable (irrespective of how much the range increases elsewhere). Range change quantifies the degree of range size difference of future divided by current size (*100). A range change of 150% means that ranges are larger in the future than under current conditions. These statistics are usually quantified using the "current modelled" range and the "future modelled" range and not the current mapped range. To avoid overpredictions under both current and future conditions, modelled ranges are often confined to modelled maps that are confined to a buffer zone around current range maps (e.g. buffered by 500 or 1000 km; see e.g. Zurell et al. 2018, Nature Climate Change, my homepage). For all of these calculations, you first need to clarify point 2) above. You calculate one single metric for one ensemble model (or you quantify range loss and range change for each model/data combination and then you give mean and s.d. for range loss and range change across all realizations, which would carry over the uncertainty quantification into this statistic). Currently, you do neither of these statistics. Rather you take ensembled uncertainties as suitabilities and then you quantify the degree of change in uncertainty, but you interpret it as change in suitability. This will cause headache to most reviewers. We find it is not difficult for you to correct this and all follow-up analyses would be easier to explain and to quantify. 

                4) When you aggregate all pine projections (current or future) into one single global map, it gets really complex (and confusing). You somehow average/aggregate all uncertainty maps into one single global pine map (yet, we think you do not explain how you do so). Is it mean or median of all uncertainty maps? You interpret this map as "suitability for pines". But in fact it is mean uncertainty of a varying set of pines per pixel (and between current and future, the set of pines that build this uncertainty may change). Your interpretation and long discussion of this global map seems wrong to us (We are sorry to say). First, it is not very informative as main result. It says how certain the projections are, on average, across all present species, right? Or are the number of species somehow integrated into this measure? The question here is, how did you treat species that are absent from a pixel (have a value of 0 for uncertainty of presence); from the maps we think to interpret that you neglect them for calculating mean(median)? uncertainties... The real problem discussing these maps come from mixing species with very different ecologies. One extreme example of this problem is where you discuss Chihuahuan desert pines' response to climate change. You state that their suitabiliy decreases (infact its unclear if the suitability increases, but the certainty of finding any of the species decreases according to your way of calculating the map). Then you proceed to say that there is escape room for them at lower latitudes (so in subtropical regions) in the future. This is grossly neglecting the fact that these lower latitudes are inhabited by completely different, subtropical, species (which tend to have less reduction in their projection certainties). But these regions are by no means an escape room for desert pines. Their ecology does not allow them to inhabit subtropical climates. Your MRCA analyses do quantify to what degree pines can shift their ranges. But much of your presentation and discussion of results is based on this global mean presence uncertainty of pine map, which mixes completely different ecologies and does NOT respect the MRCA analyses you have done. We think that much of the presentation of results and of the discussion should be adjusted. If you correct 2 (for how you calculate range loss/change), you could map, in absolute or relative terms, how many of the species that inhabit a specieic pixel now are projected to experience severe range loss; or range reduction vs. range gain. There are many possibilities on how to aggregate results, in fact, and most would be more informative than an "average presence uncertainty" map... 

                5) rather minor points: AUC says random model fit at a value of 0.5, while Kappa and TSS say random model fit at a value of 0. You cannot say that values for all measures above 0.75 are good models. It depends on the metric used. AUC values should always be higher than Kappa/TSS.
